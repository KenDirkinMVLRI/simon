Strategic diagnosis

Sources, assumptions, and toy-model limits

This is a strategy game, not a forecast. Resource units are normalized game units inspired by public data. The point is to feel the trade-off space: shifting demand helps, but large AI buildouts usually require additive supply, cooling, grid capacity, transparency, and public legitimacy.

Calibration anchors used to set the game’s shape

  • IEA, Energy demand from AI: global data-center electricity consumption is projected to roughly double to about 945 TWh by 2030 in the Base Case, just under 3% of global electricity consumption.
  • IEA, Energy supply for AI: data-center electricity is physically supplied by local grids, with renewables, gas, coal, and nuclear all relevant; fossil fuels remain important in near-term demand spikes.
  • Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory, 2024 U.S. Data Center Energy Usage Report: U.S. data centers used 176 TWh in 2023, about 4.4% of U.S. electricity consumption, with a 2028 scenario range of roughly 325–580 TWh.
  • The same LBNL report estimated substantial indirect water consumption from electricity generation for U.S. data centers, making cooling method, location, and grid mix important.
  • U.S. EIA, Use of energy for transportation: light-duty vehicles account for the largest share of U.S. transportation-sector energy use, motivating the game’s transportation trade-off sliders.

Game-design choices

Digital media savings are intentionally modest compared with transport, HVAC peaks, industrial flexibility, and additive power builds. “Carbon headroom” represents emissions space under a social or policy constraint, not a license to emit. “Energy headroom” mixes electricity, avoided peak load, and fuel-equivalent flexibility because the game is about prioritization rather than exact engineering dispatch.
